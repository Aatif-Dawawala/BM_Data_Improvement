Every blessing is a test. 
In ease or hardship, Allah examines our hearts, will gratitude blossom, or will arrogance take root?
These verses from Surah Al-Baqarah (2:49–60) tell the story of Bani Isra’il, whose journey from slavery to freedom mirrors our own struggle to stay grateful and faithful.
Allah saved the Children of Israel from Pharaoh’s tyranny, a ruler who killed their sons and enslaved their people (Ibn Kathir, Al-Razi).
 The sea split into twelve paths for them, a miracle of mercy, yet soon after, they worshipped the golden calf. Their repentance cost them dearly (Ibn Juzay, Ma‘arif al-Qur’an).
In the desert, Allah’s care continued. Clouds shaded them, manna and salwa descended for food, and twelve springs gushed forth, one for each tribe (Ibn Kathir). But when told to enter the city humbly saying <i>Hittah</i>, they mocked and changed Allah’s words (Ma‘arif al-Qur’an).
Their story warns us that forgetting blessings leads to betrayal of the soul and distance from divine mercy.
What did Pharaoh's people do to the Children of Israel? 
Killed their sons and enslaved their women - True 
Forced them to build pyramids - False 
Made them worship Egyptian gods - False 
Explanation: Verse 49 states that Pharaoh’s people “inflicted upon you dreadful torment slaughtering your sons and sparing your women.” This shows both physical and psychological oppression, aiming to destroy their lineage and dignity. 
How many springs gushed out when Moses struck the rock? 
Twelve springs - True 
Seven springs - False 
Ten springs - False 
Explanation: Verse 60 describes, “When Moses prayed for water, We said, ‘Strike the rock with your staff,’ and twelve springs gushed forth. Each tribe knew its drinking place.” This reflects Allah’s perfect organization and mercy. 
What happened to those who demanded to see Allah directly? 
They were struck by thunderbolts and died - True 
They were turned into stone - False 
They were banished from the community - False 
Explanation: Verses 55–56 describe that when they arrogantly demanded to see Allah, “thunderbolts struck you as you looked on. Then We revived you after your death.” This teaches that faith must be through submission, not physical proof. 
According to the lesson, why are both hardship and rescue called tests? 
Success can be harder to handle than struggle - True 
Allah wants to make life difficult - False 
People need constant challenges - False 
Explanation: Both hardship and rescue are balā’ (tests). Allah tests patience through difficulty and gratitude through ease reminding us that success can be a greater trial than suffering. 
What did the Children of Israel worship while Moses was away? 
A golden calf - True 
Egyptian idols - False 
Explanation: Verse 51 says, “You took to worshipping the calf after him,” showing how quickly people can turn to shirk when faith weakens. 
What was the consequence when the Israelites mocked Allah’s command and changed His word? 
A plague was sent from the heavens - True 
They were expelled from the town - False 
Their food supply was cut off - False 
Explanation: Verse 59 states that because they “changed the word they were told,” Allah sent down a plague from the heavens as punishment for their rebellion. Mocking divine commands brings real consequence. 
How long was Moses appointed to stay on Mount Sinai? 
Forty nights - True 
Thirty nights - False 
Twelve nights - False 
Explanation: Verse 51 affirms, “We appointed forty nights for Moses,” referring to his seclusion on Mount Sinai to receive divine revelation a time of spiritual preparation for guidance.
